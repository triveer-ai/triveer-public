AssumptionSomething believed to be true but not stated or proven — hidden assumptions are where many arguments secretly fail.

BiasA mental shortcut that usually helps but sometimes causes systematic errors — not stupidity, but a built-in software bug in your brain.

Care-Based ArgumentA moral argument that emphasizes relationships, empathy, and responsibility to others.
Causal ComplexityThe reality that most effects have multiple interacting causes — necessary, sufficient, and contributing factors working together.
CausationWhen one thing directly produces another — requires a mechanism, correct time order, and no simpler alternative explanation.

Clarifying QuestionA question that sharpens vague terms — "What exactly do you mean by...?"
Concept MapA visual tool showing relationships BETWEEN ideas using labeled connection lines — shows HOW ideas relate, not just hierarchy.

Convergent ThinkingNarrowing down from many ideas to the best ones — the evaluation phase after brainstorming.
CorrelationWhen two things happen together or change together — but this alone does NOT prove one causes the other.

DebiasingActive strategies for countering cognitive biases — Consider the Opposite, Pre-mortem, Outsider Test, Base Rate Check, "Was I Wrong?" Journal.
Decision MatrixA tool for comparing multiple options against weighted criteria — rate each option 1-5 on each criterion, multiply by weights, compare totals.
Deductive ReasoningIf premises are true and logic is valid, the conclusion MUST be true — the "math of thinking" that guarantees certainty.
Deontological ArgumentA moral argument that judges actions by whether they follow moral rules — "some things are wrong regardless of the outcome."
Divergent ThinkingGenerating many ideas without judging — the creative brainstorming phase before evaluation.

OmissionWhat's deliberately left out of a message, argument, or presentation — sometimes the most important information is what's NOT said.
Optimism/Pessimism BiasSystematically over- or underestimating outcomes based on emotional state rather than evidence.

Perspective DisruptionBreaking mental loops by seeing through different eyes — explain to a beginner, reverse the problem, argue the opposite, or ask an outsider.
PMIPlus, Minus, Interesting — a quick analysis framework for evaluating any idea or proposal in under 2 minutes.
Pre-mortemImagining that a decision has already FAILED and working backwards to identify why — counteracts optimism bias.

Probing QuestionA question that digs into reasoning — "Why do you think that's true?"
Problem StatementA precise description of a problem using the template — [Who] has a problem with [what] because [root cause], which leads to [consequence].
Rational Decision ProcessAn 8-step system — Define, Identify values, Generate options, Gather info, Evaluate, Pre-mortem, Decide, Review later.
RecursionApplying a process to its own output — productive when each cycle improves, a trap when each cycle just restates.
Response LadderSix levels of disagreement from weakest (name-calling) to strongest (addressing the central point with evidence).
Reverse CausationWhen the assumed cause-and-effect direction is actually backwards — maybe B causes A, not A causes B.
Rights-Based ArgumentA moral argument focusing on fundamental, non-negotiable rights that should not be violated.
SCAMPERA creative thinking technique — Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to another use, Eliminate, Reverse.
Scientific MethodObserve → Hypothesize → Test → Conclude — the most reliable way to determine what's actually true.
SignalRelevant evidence that directly answers your question — the useful information you're looking for amid the noise.
Signal FilterThree questions — Does this directly answer my question? Is it factual or emotional? Would my conclusion change without it?
Six Thinking HatsA group discussion framework — White (facts), Red (feelings), Black (risks), Yellow (benefits), Green (ideas), Blue (process).
So What? FrameworkAfter any fact or conclusion, ask "So what?" to determine — Why does it matter? Who's affected? What should we DO?
Sound ArgumentAn argument that is both valid (logic works) AND has true premises — the only type you can fully trust.
Spotlight EffectMassively overestimating how much others notice and care about your actions or mistakes.
Status Quo BiasPreferring things to stay the same even when change might be better — comfort overriding rational evaluation.
Steel ManRebuilding someone's argument in its STRONGEST possible form before responding — the opposite of a straw man.
Straw ManAttacking a weak, distorted version of someone's argument that nobody actually made — easy to knock down but dishonest.
SubtextThe real meaning behind words — what's implied or suggested without being directly stated.
Sufficient CauseA factor that alone is enough to produce the effect — it guarantees the outcome by itself.
Sunk Cost FallacyContinuing a failing course of action because of past investment (time, money, effort) that can't be recovered.
SWOTStrengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats — a framework for evaluating personal fit for an opportunity.
SyllogismA deductive argument with two premises and a conclusion — "All A are B. X is A. Therefore X is B."
Thinking JournalA daily micro-practice template — Situation, My First Reaction, My Analyzed Response, What I Learned.
Three Laws of ThoughtIdentity (A is A), Non-Contradiction (A can't be both true and false), Excluded Middle (either A or not-A).
Three WhysAsk WHY three times to drill from surface answers to root motivations or causes.
Triangular ThinkingExamining any claim from exactly three different angles before judging — stakeholders, time lenses, or modes (emotional/logical/practical).
Valid ArgumentAn argument where the conclusion follows logically from the premises — the logic structure works, regardless of whether premises are actually true.
Virtue-Based ArgumentA moral argument that judges actions by whether they reflect good character — "What would a good person do?"
Vision StatementA personal compass — "I care about [values]. I want to [goal]. When I face a decision, I ask: does this move me toward [vision]?"

FAQ

Questions, Answered

Everything you might be wondering before you join the waitlist.

How long is a single ThinQr bite?
About 90 seconds. Each bite has 5–7 interactive cards (story, challenge, reveal, quiz, reflection). One bite fits in a coffee break; three bites fit in a commute.
Is ThinQr really free?
Yes. Trails 1–3 (16 bites, 116 cards), the full ThinQr Mind Type Assessment, the Thinking Journal, daily streaks, Think Squad, the home-screen widget, and iCloud backup are free with no card required. ThinQr Pro is a one-time $6.99 unlock for the remaining 7 trails — no subscription, no recurring charge.
What is the ThinQr Mind Type Assessment?
ThinQr Mind Type is our independent cognitive-style framework — 4 axes (Approach, Stance, Lens, Scope) producing 16 archetypes, plus a 0–100 ThinQr Score across 8 reasoning domains. Different from personality typologies like MBTI: instead of mapping how you interact with the world, it maps how you reason through it (logic, bias awareness, evidence handling, decisions). And unlike a frozen label, your ThinQr Mind Type evolves as you train — retake monthly to track growth. Every result ships with a personalized growth plan that points to the exact trails to close your gaps.
What happens if I lose my streak?
ThinQr gives you an automatic streak freeze that absorbs one missed day, so the occasional bad day doesn't reset your progress. Even if a streak does break, your longest streak is kept as a permanent record on your stats screen.
Do I need an account to use ThinQr?
No signup, no login, no account creation. Open the app and start learning immediately. Your progress stays private to your device and securely backs up to your personal iCloud — only you can see it.
Can I share my ThinQr Mind Type or scores with friends?
Yes — sharing is built into the core experience. ThinQr generates branded share cards for your ThinQr Mind Type, ThinQr Score, completed bites, streak milestones, badges earned, the weekly Brain Snap, and quiz results. One tap sends them to iMessage, Instagram Stories, Twitter, or anywhere else.
Does ThinQr track me or sell my data?
No. We use no third-party analytics SDKs, no advertising identifiers, and no behavioral trackers. Most learning apps monetize your behavior data — we cannot, because we don't collect it. Read the full policy.
Will there be an Android version?
iOS first. We're focused on a polished iOS 17+ launch. An Android version is on the roadmap once we hit our iOS launch milestones — join the waitlist to get the first Android beta invite.
